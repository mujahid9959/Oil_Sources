Soil Requirements Peanuts prefer well-drained sandy loam soils with a pH of 6.0 to 6.5. Soil should be free of compact layers to allow the pegs to penetrate and develop properly.
Climate Peanuts thrive in warm climates with temperatures ranging from 20°C to 30°C. They require a growing season of 120-150 frost-free days for optimal development.
Irrigation Needs Adequate moisture is crucial during flowering and pod development. Irrigation may be necessary in dry periods, but overwatering should be avoided to prevent root rot.
Planting Density Recommended planting density is about 100,000 to 120,000 plants per acre, with rows spaced 30 to 36 inches apart. Adequate spacing helps reduce competition for nutrients and sunlight.
Nutrient Requirements Peanuts require a balanced supply of nutrients, particularly phosphorus, potassium, and calcium. Inoculation with nitrogen-fixing bacteria can improve yields.
Pest and Disease Management Common pests include the peanut burrower bug and thrips. Diseases like leaf spot and root rot can be managed through crop rotation and resistant varieties.
Harvesting Method Peanuts are typically harvested when 70% of the leaves turn yellow, and the soil is dry. Harvesting can be done manually or with machinery, usually 4-5 months after planting.
Post-Harvest Processing After harvesting, peanuts need to be dried to reduce moisture content for safe storage. They can be sold raw, roasted, or processed into peanut butter and oil.
Market Demand Peanuts are in demand for their culinary uses, including snacks, oil production, and as ingredients in various products. The market is also influenced by health trends.
Culinary Uses Peanuts are commonly consumed as snacks (raw or roasted), used in confectionery, and made into peanut butter. They are also used in sauces, marinades, and desserts.
Health Benefits Rich in protein, healthy fats, vitamins (like E and B-complex), and minerals (like magnesium and phosphorus). Associated with heart health and weight management.
By-Products By-products from peanut processing include peanut hulls (used for animal bedding, mulch, or in bioenergy production) and peanut meal (used as animal feed).
Environmental Impact Peanuts can improve soil health through nitrogen fixation. Sustainable practices, such as crop rotation, can enhance biodiversity and soil structure.
Storage and Shelf Life Properly dried peanuts can be stored for several months to a year in a cool, dry place. Insufficiently dried peanuts may spoil or develop mold.

After Oil Extraction: Uses of Peanut By-Products

Uses of Peanut Wastage Description
Peanut Cake Used as high-protein animal feed, rich in nutrients for livestock; can also be used in aquaculture feed.
Peanut Hulls Utilized as bedding for livestock, in mushroom cultivation, or as a mulch in gardening and landscaping.
Peanut Oil Residue Can be refined for use in cosmetics and personal care products or as a flavoring agent in food products.
Peanut Meal A by-product of oil extraction, often used in animal feed, particularly for poultry and swine.
Peanut Flour Made from ground peanuts, used as a gluten-free flour alternative in baking and cooking.
Peanut Shells Ground for use in bioenergy production, as a natural abrasive in cleaning products, or for soil amendment.
Peanut Protein Isolate Extracted for use in protein supplements and food products, popular in health food and sports nutrition.
